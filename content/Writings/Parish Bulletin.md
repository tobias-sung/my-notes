# St. Carlo Acutis - The Saint Who Made A Website

![](https://cdn.britannica.com/57/251957-050-8B8B0815/carlo-acutis-italian-teen-computer-programmer-beatified-catholic-church.jpg)

> [!Quote] St. Carlo Acutis
> *"“All people are born as originals but many die as photocopies.”*

When we think of saints, we may think of impossible ideals. Spirtual heroes who accomplished great deeds and endured much suffering for the love of God. So it was with some surprise when I learned of the canonization of St. Carlo Acutis, the first millenial saint. His claim to fame? He made a website.

Obviously, that's a massive understatement. But as I learned more about him, what struck me the most was how ordinary he was on the outside. He was born to a middle class family, enjoyed video games and surfing the Web, and did well at school. The most unusual thing about him is his devotion to the Church at such a young age.

He became a catechist at the age of 14, and spent hours in Eucharistic adoration when other kids his age would rather be anywhere else (he famously called the Eucharist "the motorway to heaven"). He was very kind to others, lending an ear to the troubles of his classmates and helping the poor whenever he could. And he had a desire to spread the faith to anyone who would receive it. That was what inspired him to create a website documenting Eucharistic miracles (in which praying to the Eucharist led to incredible happenigns), in the hopes that historical evidence presented on such a far-reaching platform could draw more people to the Church. 

It's embarassing for me to admit this, but my first reaction was: That's it? He was kind to everyone, spent a lot of time at Church and then made a website?

He didn't fight in battles like St. Joan of Arc. He didn't found a renowned religious order like St. Igantius of Loyola. He didn't travel to distant lands to spread the faith like St. Joseph Freinademtz. 

But he still was canonized as a saint, and so soon after his death! (He passed away in 2006 from leukemia, not too long after his website launched) Was this simply a ploy by the Church to pander to the youth? 

At this point, it became clear that I had a very *wrong* definition of what it means to be a saint. Saints are not superheroes. They're not impossible ideals. They are simply good people who had faith in God. While the most memorable saints certainly performed extraordinary feats of faith, a large number of them lived "unremarkable" lives. Like St. Therese of Liseaux, who spent most of her short life cloistered away in a convent. Or St. Alphonsius Rodrigeuz, who was a humble doorman at a Jesuit college for more than 40 years. 

They remind us that great spiritual feats are not outwardly impressive. They are simple acts of love that often go unnoticed. And just because they are simple, it doesn't mean that they are easy. But they are within reach.

The canonization of St. Carlo Acutis is an invitation to all of us to aspire to be saints. And to regard the Internet, this terrible and wonderful invention that we too often take for granted, as a sacred gift from God to be used for the good of His Will. St. Carlo Acutis, pray for us!
