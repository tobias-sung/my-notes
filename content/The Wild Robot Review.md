The Wild Robot is not a film about artificial intelligence. Most stories about AI are never really commenting on the ethical issues or technical possibilities of real-world AI, but rather use it as a vehicle to explore the intricacies of human nature. Similar to time travel stories, you're not supposed to think too hard about the mechanics behind the technology. If the story's successful, you'll be so swept up in the experience that you won't even think about all the things that don't make sense. The Wild Robot more than succeeds. It easily ranks among Dreamwork's best films, transcending its bog-standard plot to be more than the sum of its parts. More than it was programmed to be.

# You Don't Understand Anything

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **Brightbill**: I still won't belong.
> 
> **Roz**: I understand.
> 
> **Brightbill**: You don't understand anything! You don't feel anything!

Note that when I talk about AI, I'm referring to the *fictional* idea of AI. Like most of the general public, I have no idea how AI actually works. I have *heard* about two types of AI: *generative* artificial intelligence and artificial *general* intelligence. Easy to confuse, I know. One is very real, while the other is more theoretical. 

Generative AI is obviously the hottest thing right now, with everyone and their mother using ChatGPT for just about anything you can imagine. One of the biggest fears surrounding gen-AI is how it will impact culture. From voice acting to generating entire films, studio executives are salivating at how much money they could potentially save by creating million dollar hits out of nothing more than prompts. What if you could just tell a gen-AI to make an animated film based on the bestselling book "The Wild Robot" instead of waiting 4 years for hundreds of creatives to painstakingly handcraft the final product? For now, that's just a dream. Gen-AI is nowhere near that level, at least as far as I know. This [article](https://www.forbes.com/sites/bernardmarr/2024/05/08/the-important-difference-between-generative-ai-and-agi/) from Forbes has a great quote on the limitations of gen-AI using a topic that's quite relevant to The Wild Robot: love.

![[Emotion.png]]

Gen-AI cannot understand or feel love. It can draw on an enormous database of words and phrases that describe love or process billions of images depicting expressions of love (of which there are many, perhaps *too many*). And even after analyzing all this data and identifying all the patterns, it can still only provide an *approximation* of love. It's an incredibly advanced parrot, but like a parrot it doesn't know what it's saying. 

Artificial general intelligence, on the other hand, is much closer to what science fiction has envisioned AI to be: a machine that can learn and reason the way humans do. It doesn't just view everything as cold, hard data, but can *understand* the meaning and context behind the input it is receiving. Therefore, it can adapt something it learned and apply it in a completely different context, which is simple for humans but mind-blowing for a machine. 

The following quote from AI researcher Pei Wang, which may as well be a summary of the The Wild Robot, provides a simple definition of what it means for a machine to learn like a human: "the ability for an information processing system to adapt to its environment with **insufficient** knowledge and resources." 

![[Dropped Into The Wild.png]]

The Wild Robot is the story of a machine that has been dropped into an environment it was never trained to inhabit. A machine that, instead of shutting down, carefully observes the whole of nature, absorbing both its violence and beauty, to come up with an entirely new way of survival. This isn't ChatGPT simply crunching data to approximate an idea of something, but a true intelligence slowly (and messily) cultivating a deep understanding of its surroundings to reach a conclusion about the world.
# Making Stuff Up

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Rummage**: Can you explain again what we are doing?
> **Roz**: I DON'T KNOW! I’m just...making stuff up! I don't know what I'm doing and I have to! I have to because he's relying on me!

A computer does not make mistakes. Every time our software crashes we might blame the computer, but the truth is that the computer is simply executing what it was told to do. It's the human programmers who make mistakes. Computers are perfect. Humans are not.

Although ROZZUM Unit 7134 starts out the film looking like a fool, she is still technically "perfect". Her voice is cheerful and optimistic, never changing in pitch. She does exactly what she was programmed to do: keeping herself alive and seeking out tasks. She never goes off script, never does anything outside of her operational boundaries. But through a series of mishaps, she ends up acquiring a task that she was never designed for: being a mother. A task that is complex, messy and ambiguous. 

And in order to complete this task, the once-perfect ROZZUM Unit 7134 has to become increasingly imperfect. Increasingly human.

![[Ambiguity.png]]

Being human is an incredibly messy and frustrating affair. Yet our race, in all its stupid glory, somehow stumbled into being the most advanced civilization on the planet. And it's because we have this strange ability of developing good ideas. But where do good ideas come from? No one knows for sure, but what we do know is that they don't come easily. It's a messy and inefficient process of failure and self-doubt. And at the end of it all, it's hard to define exactly how you even got there. You could probably trace a series of incidents or logical statements but sooner or later, you start making unexplainable leaps that don't make sense yet somehow lead to something incredible. To put it simply, you just start making stuff up.

A computer would never be able to handle that. Ambiguity is a computer's worst nightmare. The perfect cannot process the imperfect. But it's only through grappling with ambiguity that anything new can be born.

# Eat, Swim, Fly By Fall

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **ROZZUM 7134**: Eat. Swim. Fly by fall. Or aaaaugh.
> 
> **Pinktail:** Patience is the key.
>

ROZZUM Unit 7134, faced with a task she does not have the programming for, asks a real mother for advice: a weary opossum named Pinktail who's probably given birth to more litters than the number of years she's lived. Not wanting to confuse the new mother, Pinktail breaks the task down into three simple steps: make sure the gosing eats, swims and can fly by fall. 

Initially this sounds perfect for the robot, as clean and unambiguous as an instruction manual. 

Eat. 

Swim. 

Fly by fall. 

But of course, Pinktail only focused on the *physical* aspects of raising a child, conveniently neglecting to mention all the *emotional* needs a child has. 

![[Emotional.png]]

It's easy for Unit 7134 to build a home and find food for her gosling (with a few pointers from the crafty Fink, a fox who takes advantage of Unit 7134's naivete to get free food and protection). But she struggles with more "impractical" matters, like coming up with a name for the kid. After coming up with such creative names like "0001" and "0186", she finally settles on Brightbill because she observes that the gosling's bill is rather...bright. In a similar way, Brightbill also gives Unit 7134 a new name: Roz. Because his adorable little mouth can't pronounce his mother's full name, only able to spit out "Roz" followed by incomprehensible babble.

Then there's the matter of putting Brightbill to sleep. Unlike a machine, there's no off button on the little guy (how all parents wish there was...) Fink suggests that Roz tell him a bedtime story, but at this point Roz doesn't have the ability to make things up yet. So Fink performs an incredibly cheesy re-telling of Roz and Brightbill's meeting, conveniently leaving out some rather uncomfortable truths. Like the part where Roz killed Brightbill's entire family in an accident. Or how she only adopted Brightbill because she was "given" a task by Pinktail. What matters in the end is that Brightbill finally goes to sleep.

![[Sleep.png]]

Now, Roz simply has to feed and put Brightbill to sleep every day. And also start training him to swim and fly by fall. Little does she know, the learning process for living beings is very different from her own. 

For Roz, learning a new *physical* skill is simple. She only needs to observe something once before being able to mimic it. Whether it be climbing sideways like a crab, or bounding gracefully through the trees like a deer, the learning process is immediate and painless. But for living beings, it is anything but.

 Learning means overcoming the pain of failure multiple times just to attain the most basic level of competency at something. Failing to manage our emotions, we end up giving up and making no progress. Adults (mostly) can learn things on their own because they have the *emotional intelligence* to deal with failure. But a newborn child is still running off the most basic programming of avoiding pain. When they inevitably fail, they won't push through unless they have the guidance and encouragement of an adult.

![[Guidance.png]]

At the beginning of their relationship, both Roz and Brightbill lack emotional intelligence. In Roz's case, it's because she doesn't have any emotions. For Brightbill, it's because he has too many. Yet somehow Roz has to get Brightbill through the very emotional process of learning to grow up, despite the fact that she herself does not even understand emotions.

So in order for Roz to successfully complete her task, she has to do that cliched thing that all robots do in stories like this: learn how to *feel*. And for Roz, learning how to feel will turn out to be just as difficult as it is for us living things to learn anything.
# You Should Not Feel Anything At All

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Rummage**: May I run a diagnostic? 
> 
> **Roz**: I feel fine.
> 
> **Rummage**: You should not *feel* anything at all.

(Please note that I'm not a neuroscientist, so everything I'm about to say in the following section is just a monkey trying to comprehend something it can't fully understand.)

The most fundamental component for human intelligence is the neuron, a type of cell in our brain that is responsible for sending and receiving messages. When a neuron sends a message, we tend to say that it "fires", like a gun shooting out a bullet, presumably because of the incredible speed at which these mesages travel. For years scientists have learned how the brain works by putting test subjects into specific situations and identifying which neurons are "fired". For example, if a lot of neurons in a certain area of the brain "fire" when we move our arms, that area is probably related to motor function. 

Neurons can serve many types of functions. One type that has gotten a lot of attention is the "mirror neuron". Put simply, mirror neurons fire exactly the same way under two different circumstances: when we observe an action, and when we perform the same action ourselves. So, as an example, our brain responds the same way whether *we* are eating or watching *someone else* eat.  

While there's nothing conclusive about it yet, there are a lot of theories suggesting that mirror neurons could be the key to learning, empathy, complex language and even self-awareness. In short, it might be the basis for our humanity (a really bold claim that still requires more evaluation).

At the most basic level, mirror neurons allow us to, you guessed it, "mirror" the actions of another person. For example, if you do a silly face or stick your tongue out at an infant, there's a chance that the baby will copy your facial expression.

![[Mirror1.png]]

But the theory is that when we "mirror" someone, we go beyond mere copying. Somehow, we understand the intention or emotion behind that action, and learn how to use it ourselves. 

So if you're a parent and you spend a lot of time smiling at your new little human, the baby might smile back at you despite not understanding what the smile means. But then, the act of smiling somehow triggers a positive feeling in their stupid little head. Therefore, they learn to associate smiling with positive emotion. The baby understands that whenever their parent is smiling, it's because they are happy. And so the baby learns to express its own joy through a smile.

![[Cute.png]]

In short, mirror neurons allow us to understand what other people are feeling. We can observe an action and instinctively know (and feel) the associated emotion.

If we watch someone getting their teeth pulled out at the dentist, it somehow feels unbearable. If we watch a cockroach crawl up someone's arm, our own skin crawls. If we observe someone crying, we might feel the urge to cry ourselves.

The same holds true when we watch films, which (if they're doing their job) makes us feel as if we have stepped into the shoes of the characters. The best action films make us feel the impact of every punch. The best dramas make us feel the weight of each relationship. The best horror films fill us with the dread of impending death (even if we are in the safety of our own home). 

So what does all this have to do with The Wild Robot? Well, the second I saw Roz observe a crab climbing up a cliff sideways, then immediately mimicking it, the idea of "mirror neurons" instantly popped into my head. 

![[Mimic1.png]]

If Roz can perfectly mirror the action of another living being, can she understand the emotion driving that action through her mimicry?

Brightbill, upon first hatching, takes his first glimpse of the world and what he sees are the gleaming blue eyes of ROZZUM Unit 7134. And at that moment, as far as he's concerned, Roz *is* the entire world. And, in the purest expression of love, he presses his little face against hers. But is it really love? We later learn that goslings imprint on the first thing they see (real-life geese *have* been shown to literally "fall in love" with something as silly as a scientist's boots). While their "reason" might be programmed into them, I believe the resulting emotion is still quite real. I believe in that one simple, earth-shattering moment, Brightbill was programatically expressing a very real love. And Roz, somehow, feels it too. 

![[Pure Expression of Love.png]]

It's important that this isn't the very first animal that Roz encountered on the Island. So far, every single animal has either run away from her or tried to murder her. Although Roz is still emotionally bare, she knows enough to understand these reactions to be negative. [Just like how a cat feels threatened even if you yell profanities at it that it cannot understand](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=23Te7QAsH6w). But along comes Brightbill, who does something that no other animal has ever done before: voluntarily engaging in prolonged physical contact. Even if Roz doesn't know what love is, she can understand that there's something special about this, something completely *new*. It causes her entire body to glow brilliantly in a way it has never done before, her core exploding into colorful shapes, as if some beautiful defect has compromised her entire system.

![[Glow.png]]

And for the rest of her life, Roz will associate this simple action with this strange sensation.

Near the end of the film, when Roz has finally "learned" how to feel, we see her "mirror" the same action, except this time with complete understanding and acceptance of what it means. Even the shot itself is a literal mirror of the first one.

![[Mimic.png]]

The Wild Robot doesn't waste time on the question of, "Will the robot learn how to feel?" We already know she will. The filmmakers instead put all their efforts into telling the story of *how* the robot learns to feel, to ensure that it happened in a natural and organic way. 

And it feels real because Roz learns the same way we learn, through the act of "mirroring". Or, as the saying goes, "fake it till you make it". 

Roz's programming compels her to complete the task of raising Brightbill, but she doesn't have the programming to be a mother. What she is programmed to do is to mimic, to pretend. And so she pretends to be a mother, doing all the things that other mothers are expected to do without fully understanding it. Building a nest, finding food, lying about the uncomfortable truths of our harsh and unforgiving reality... You know, typical mom stuff. And in the process of pretending to be a mother, Roz eventually *becomes* a mother, acquiring all the emotional baggage that comes with such a role.   

I love how there's no clear cut-off point where you can say, "Oh, this is *the* moment where Roz becomes a mother." There's no clear turning point where her voice just starts to distinctly become more human (an idea which was in the original script but thankfully changed in the final performance). Rather, there's a weird back and forth that goes on throughout most of the film where Roz can't seem to figure out what she is.

![[Confused.png]]

She refuses to refer to Brightbill as her son most of the time, and when he accuses her of taking care of him out of guilt she defensively says that she needed a task. 

Later, Brightbill verbally tells her that her task is over and that she can just leave, which means that her programming should no longer oblige her to take care of him (it's not in the script, but her heads-up-display briefly flashes red when Brightbill says this, hinting at some kind of glitch). Yet she just doubles down and puts in even more effort getting him in the air. 

On the day he is to leave on migration and they are likely to be parted forever, Roz seems cold and distant. "I guess it's time," Brightbill says. "Yes," Roz replies. "Will you be here when I get back?" He asks. "No," she says without elaborating. Yet when he does finally take off, and she checks off the final item on her task list, she suddenly rushes to the edge of the island just to get a final glimpse of him.

Is she taking care of Brightbill out of obligation from her programming, or is it something more? Does her joy come from checking requirements off of a list or seeing her child grow stronger? Is her inability to verbally express love because of a genuine lack of it, or because she is afraid of being vulnerable? What makes Roz's struggle feel so compelling is the fact that real humans with real hearts can also suffer from these doubts. 

![[Conflict.png]]

As mentioned before, a generative AI can only "fake it" but can never "make it". It can mimic love, but it will never gain an understanding of love through its mimicry. It can study billions of images of facial expressions to approximate empathy, but it can never be truly empathic. But Roz is not AI. She's us. We start off faking the person we're trying to be, and then one day we realize we've been that person for longer than we've thought. We can't point to that exact moment when things changed, when the role became reality. It just kind of happened along the way. 
# Funny, How Life Works

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Longneck**: The accident that killed your family, saved you.
> **Brightbill**: I-I never thought of it that way.
> **Longneck**: Funny, how life works.

As the famous saying goes, we live life forwards but can only understand it backwards. Looking back, the mess of our lives somehow coheres into something beautiful even if we were just trying to get by every day. 

Roz was never meant to arrive on the Island, but she was stranded there after a typhoon crashed her ship. She then killed Brightbill's entire family in an accident, and somehow it was Brightbill, the runt with the least chances of surviving, that did. She went on to become his mother, not by choice but because a simple turn of phrase from Pinktail triggered her task-acquirement programming. She happened upon Fink the fox, who started off trying to eat Brightbill only to later switch gears, pretending to help Roz for his own benefit, before ultimately becoming the third member of this weird little family.

![[Family.png]]

Life is a series of coincidences and chance encounters. Even the most advanced computer model that has been fed the entirety of all human experience could not predict with 100% accuracy the twists and turns of an individual life. It's too random, too imperfect. 

# What You Did To Try And Fix It

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Brightbill:** What happened was not your fault. But what you did to try and fix it, is everything.

Although Roz is technically the "hero" of the story, her presence on the Island turns out to have more than a few negative repercussions.

Upon her arrival, she disrupts the environment with her noisy advertising and destructive attempts at "helping" the local animals (from handing out free stickers to destroying a beaver's dam). 

 ![[Disruption.png]]

Her clumsiness eventually leads to an accident that kills an entire family of geese. Although she raises the sole survivor as her own child, she passes on some of her own robotic nature to him, which causes him to be an outcast among his own people. She wants to protect her son by hiding the truth about his family, only causing him more pain when he inevitably finds out.

![[Accident.png]]

And worst of all, by activating her transmitter she alerts the humans of the location of the Island, which leads to a massive forest fire at the climax of the film. And opens up the possibility of them returning in the future to cause more damage.

![[Fire.png]]

The thing is, Roz isn't directly responsible for all the terrible things that happen in the film, but at the same time none of these things would have happened if she never came to the Island. 

Roz can't help but be the product of a destructive system. A system that destroyed most of the world for the sake of comfort and profit. ("Can this world get any better?", asks an advertisment for ROZZUM units...in a world that has been mostly submerged underwater). Roz isn't responsible for this destruction, but she owes her "life" to the system that caused it. And yet, in the face of this uncomfortable truth, she still tries to fix it anyway. She killed Brightbill's family but chose to raise him. The same technological progress that created her also led to the blizzard that killed many of the Island's inhabitants, and yet she still uses her advanced technology to save the survivors and keep them alive through the long winter. At the end of the film, she knows going back to her creators won't solve everything but at least it will keep the Island safe for now.

![[Underwater.png]]

Brightbill tells Roz that what happened to his family was not her fault. But what she did to try and fix it was "everything". He doesn't tell her that she *fixed* everything. Brightbill will always be a little broken, a little out of place. The world is forever scarred by the work of her creators. It's the fact that she tried that *is* everything.

As humans, we are also products of a destructive system. Especially those of us fortunate enough to enjoy middle-class lives in first-world cities. We aren't *directly* responsible for the damage being done to the world, but at the same time we can live the way we do *because* of it. It's an uncomfortable truth that we have to ignore just to live with ourselves. We might say that this destruction started long before we were born, and that we are too small to do anything about it. And it's true, we can't fix everything. The damage has been done, and continues to be done. What's the use if I cut down my electricity usage? What's the point of reducing the amount of trash we produce? Big corporations still produce millions of useless products and the rich still live disgustingly wasteful lives. 

We can't fix everything. But trying to fix it...Well, that's everything. No matter how stupid and imperfect that might be. It's better than taking the easy way out, living in mindless comfort.

# More than we were programmed to be 

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **Roz**: I know you all have instincts that keep you alive. But sometimes, to survive, we must become more than we were programmed to be.

Programming is a set of clearly defined rules. For example, a lot of computer programs just boil down to a lot of if-statements. If this happens, do that. If that happens, do this. 

But if something unexpected happens, and the program goes through all its if-statements and can't find any corresponding action...it dies. 

Okay, "crashes" is probably the more accurate term, but from the perspective of a computer program that is basically death. A program only lives as long as it is running. Unless the programmer has hastily added in a routine for handling exceptions, although this usually boils down to just resetting the program which is, once again, a kind of death.

Living beings are also programmed to do certain things, under certain circumstances. *How* exactly we are programmed is a matter of scientific debate. The question of *who* programmed us is a matter of theological debate. But what we do know is that unlike a computer program, our "code" is not fixed. Indeed, our "code" is being rewritten every single day whether we realize it or not. 

A lot of the time we are being re-written by forces outside of ourselves. Whether it's the big corporations running the Internet, the expectations of society or the people who surround us every day. Or maybe our "code" becomes corrupted when functions that once served us well in our prehistoric environment start going haywire in the modern world, like when our sensitivity to threats (which would have been very useful in a wilderness filled with predators) causes us to become eaten up by nothing even in the midst of a safe and comfortable world.

![[Re-programming.png]]

Yet we also have the ability to override our programming on our own, and the key is being *aware*. To stop and think. Acting according to our programming is the same as acting without thinking. This can be a good thing, such as when we "program" ourselves to develop good habits and start doing healthy things every day without thinking. It can also be harmful, such as when we fall into an emotional downward spiral and get stuck in loops of negative thought patterns. We can always get out of it if we can just stop and think things through, but the problem is that the simple act of thinking can be quite hard.

![[Think.png]]

Sometimes when I read the comments of film reviews on YouTube, I see commenters joking that they're so happy that the reviewer released this video so that they can finally know what to think about a film. But I suspect that they are only half-joking. Have we all been programmed into content consumption machines that receive so much input every day that we are too fatigued to produce any original thought? Is this why AI creation tools are so popular now? At the click of a button we can instantly get the "pride" of creating something rather than having to go through the arduous process of making the mess in our heads into something tangibly real.

Being what we were programmed to be means taking the easy route. Had Roz done what was expected of her, she would have activated her transmitter and returned to the factory, where she would have been assigned to a life where she always knew exactly what she had to do, in a clean and sterile environment. She would never have had to suffer everything she goes through in this film. She wouldn't have to go through the physical pain of being beaten and violated by wild animals, or the emotional pain of watching her child fly away with so much left unsaid. She wouldn't make so many mistakes as she stumbled through an environment that she was never meant to inhabit.  

But she didn't do what was expected of her. By the end of the film, her body is covered in scars and wildflowers, and yet she is more beautiful than ever. Roz tells the animals what she's learned after the long and winding road she travelled to raise Brightbill: "Sometimes to survive, we have to become more than we were programmed to be." But I think what she's really thinking about is less about surviving and more about living. Living to our fullest potential.

# I Need A Drink

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> *Brightbill smiles, and falls asleep.* 
> 
> **Roz:** The story worked. 
> 
> **Fink:** (gagging) Blech, ugh. I need a drink.

Films are artificial constructs, nothing more than blinking lights trying to convince you that they are reality. Modern animated films like The Wild Robot are literally *programmed* on computers. 

These artificial constructs are designed to get a very real emotional reaction out of you. If a film succeeds, you become emotional without even realizing it. But the moment you become aware that a film is trying to get you to feel something, the magic is lost and the only thing you feel is the desire to get your money back.   

In the "post-modern" era, some films get around this using something called "self-awareness". They basically turn a "bug" into a "feature" by intentionally making the audience aware that they are watching a film. This can be used to great effect, inviting the audience to reflect on their relationship with cinema. But then there are times when it feels like the filmmaker is pointing at a plot contrivance and saying, "Hey, this doesn't make any sense! Haha! We're so clever. Hopefully our cleverness distracts you from the fact that we're doing it anyway!" Sometimes this can be unbearably annoying, a lazy excuse for bad writing. But sometimes, perhaps if the viewer is as gullible as I am, it kind of works? Because this is exactly what The Wild Robot does. 

The scene where Fink tells Brightbill a bedtime story is almost played as a joke. Fink tries to get Roz to play along with his incredibly corny story, setting up situations where she can say something sweet or heartfelt, but Roz keeps taking things literally and pointing out things that don't make sense. It's like a parent watching a kid's film with their child and being a total killjoy with their cynical commentary.

![[Bedtime Story.png]]

In the story, a lonely goslings asks the heavens to give him a mom, and somehow a beautiful robot, who happens to be sitting on the edge of a star, hears him. Roz points out that sound can't travel through the vacuum of space, but an irritated Fink insists that she still heard Brightbill because "*she was listening with a different part of herself. A part that up to that point, she didn’t even know she had. Her heart.*" (Blech.)

Roz continues to point out things that don't make sense in Fink's story or interrupts with hilariously cold statements. It feels like the filmmakers are winking at the adults in the audience, saying, "Yeah, we know this whole thing is emotionally manipulative and corny. Just roll with it, don't ruin it for the kids." 

![[vlcsnap-2025-08-02-16h08m37s806.png]]

The thing is, this "silly" bedtime story ends up being, more or less, exactly how the film ends.

After being kidnapped onto a spaceship by the villainess VONTRA, Roz has her memories wiped and is shut down. Brightbill discovers her lifeless body and crawls into his favorite position on her neck. He tearfully tells her, "I love you, Mom", the first time he has called her that since their argument. 

![[Tomb.png]]

Roz is dead. There's no way she could possibly hear him. Yet somehow, Roz comes back to life and tells him in a tender voice, "I love you too." Later on, Roz tells Brightbill: "*They cut my power. But I still heard you. Because I was listening with a different part of myself.*"

Never mind the fact that the film just did an "I love you"/"I love you too" scene. It even repeats the "corny" line from earlier in the film with a completely straight face! With none of the irony or sarcasm. 

Yet, as the music swells and the glowing hexagons start lighting up all around Roz's core, bathing Brightbill in a warm, loving glow... What should be a plot contrivance somehow turns into a miracle.

![[Miracle.png]]

A miracle. That's what we call a "plot contrivance" in real life. When the blind see, the deaf hear and the dead inexplicably come back to life. Of course, most of us don't believe in miracles. Yet the best films can present moments that don't make sense and make even the non-religious temporarily throw out all reason and feel something powerful, something that can't be explained.

In the filmmaker's commentary, they note that Roz's mysterious "glowing core" never gets explained and doesn't make sense, but they never got any notes about it (presumably from producers or test screenings).  In their words, "...*we get emotionally swept up in it...like, something happened. And it was...magic.*" 

And that's just it. I've struggled to put into words why The Wild Robot gets away with telling a predictable story that makes unexplainable leaps and uses cheap tricks to manipulate the audience's emotions. And if you read the negative reviews of this film, for some people it definitely did not get away with it. But it did for me, and a lot of the other people who watched this film. Discarding the possibility that I'm just an easily manipulated "normie", it can only be what the filmmakers said in their commentary: magic. The mix of beautiful animation, stirring music and voice actors going way too hard for a kid's movie result in something that becomes more than an artificial construct. It has a soul.

![[Hug.png]]

It's easy to become cynical and boil down every single story into a string of familiar story blocks. At this moment, we have the "Big Argument". Then we put in the "Main Character Mopes About" moment. Finally hit the audience with an "Emotional Reconciliation". They won't see that coming, won't they? 

Maybe that's how gen-AI views storytelling. A bunch of preset story blocks that it can arrange in an endless number of combinations, and while it can't create genuinely moving "art" at the moment, I imagine it will soon have so much data at its disposal that it will produce an incredible story at some point (without understanding how).

The Wild Robot is a film that is made up of familiar story blocks, but assembled by experienced human storytellers with a deep understanding of what makes these blocks special. But they also put in some strange things without really understanding why. They make unexplainable leaps that don't make sense but somehow lead to something beautiful. Simply because it *feels* right. And that's just something that AI can't do...yet.
