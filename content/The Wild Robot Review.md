Films are artificial constructs, nothing more than blinking lights trying to convince you that they are reality. Modern animated films like The Wild Robot are literally *programmed* on computers. 

These artificial constructs are designed to get a real emotional reaction out of you. If a film succeeds, you become emotional without even realizing it. But the moment you become aware that a film is trying to get you to feel something, the magic is lost and the only thing you feel is the desire to get your money back.

For me, The Wild Robot succeeded. It easily ranks among Dreamworks' best films, transcending its familiar story to be much more than just an artificial construct.

# You Don't Understand Anything

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **Roz**: I understand.
> 
> **Brightbill**: You don't understand anything! You don't feel anything!

Like most of the general public, I have no idea how artificial intelligence actually works. I have *heard* about two types of AI: *generative* artificial intelligence and artifical *general* intelligence. Easy to confuse, I know. One is very real, while the other is more theoretical.  

Generative AI is obviously the hottest thing right now, with everyone and their mother using ChatGPT for just about anything you can imagine. One of the biggest fears surrounding gen-AI is how it will impact the film industry. From replacing voice actors to generating entire films, studio executives are salivating at how much money they could potentially save by creating million dollar hits out of nothing more than prompts. What if you could just tell a gen-AI to make an animated film based on the bestselling book "The Wild Robot" instead of waiting 4 years for hundreds of creatives to painstakingly handcraft the final product? For now, that's just a dream. Gen-AI is currently nowhere near that level, at least as far as I know. This [article](https://www.forbes.com/sites/bernardmarr/2024/05/08/the-important-difference-between-generative-ai-and-agi/) from Forbes has a great quote on the limitations of gen-AI using a topic that's quite relevant to The Wild Robot: love.

![[Emotion.png]]

Gen-AI cannot understand or feel love. It can draw on an enormous database of words and phrases that describe love or process billions of images depicting expressions of love (of which there are many, perhaps *too many*). And after analyzing all this data and identifying all the patterns, it can only *predict* what might evoke love. It's an incredibly advanced parrot, but like a parrot it doesn't know what it's saying. 

Artificial general intelligence, on the other hand, is much closer to what science fiction has envisioned AI to be: a machine that can learn and reason the way humans do. It doesn't just view everything as cold, hard data, but can *understand* the meaning and context behind the input it is receiving. Therefore, it can adapt something it learned and apply it in a completely different context, which is simple for humans but mindblowing for a machine. 

The following quote from AI researcher Pei Wang, which may as well be a summary of the The Wild Robot, provides a simple definition of what it means for a machine to learn like a human: "the ability for an information processing system to adapt to its environment with **insufficient** knowledge and resources." 

![[Dropped Into The Wild.png]]

The Wild Robot is the story of a machine that has been dropped into an environment it was never trained to inhabit. A machine that, instead of shutting down, carefully observes the whole of nature, absorbing both its violence and beauty, to come up with an entirely new way of survival. This isn't ChatGPT simply crunching data to approximate an idea of something, but a true intelligence slowly (and messily) cultivating a deep understanding of its surroundings to reach a conclusion about the world.
# Making Stuff Up

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Rummage**: Can you explain again what we are doing?
> **Roz**: I DON'T KNOW! I’m just...making stuff up! I don't know what I'm doing and I have to! I have to because he's relying on me!

A computer does not make mistakes. Every time our software crashes we might blame the computer, but the truth is that the computer is simply executing what it was told to do. It's the human programmers who make mistakes. Computers are perfect. Humans are not.

Although ROZZUM 7134 starts out looking like a fool, she is still technically "perfect". Her voice is cheerful and optimistic, never changing in pitch. She does exactly what she was programmed to do: keeping herself alive and seeking out tasks. She never goes off script, never does anything outside of her operational boundaries. But through a series of mishaps, she ends up acquiring a task that she was never designed for: raising a child. A task that is complex, messy and ambiguous. 

And in order to complete this task, the once-perfect ROZZUM 7134 has to become increasingly imperfect. Increasingly human.

![[Ambiguity.png]]

Being human is an incredibly messy and frustrating affair. Yet our race, in all its stupid glory, somehow stumbled into being the most advanced civilization on the planet. And it's because we have this strange ability of developing good ideas. But where do good ideas come from? No one knows for sure, but what we do know is that they don't come easily. It's a messy and inefficient process of failure and self-doubt. And at the end of it all, it's hard to define exactly how you even got there. You could probably trace a series of incidents or logical statements but sooner or later, you start making unexplainable leaps that don't make sense yet somehow lead to something incredible. To put it simply, you just start making stuff up.

A computer would never be able to handle that. Ambiguity is a computer's worst nightmare. The perfect cannot process the imperfect. But it's only through grappling with ambiguity that anything new can be born.

For example, how does one come up with a name? A name is meant to distinguish something from everything else. In computer systems, pieces of data are often identified by a string of numbers and characters. They don't need to mean anything, they just have to be unique. Yet for humans, we don't care so much about whether a name is unique. What's more important is whether it *feels* right (consider how we humans have this weird intuition about whether someone ["looks like their name"](https://www.thecut.com/2017/02/yes-you-really-do-look-like-your-name.html)).  

ROZZUM 7134's gosling struggles to pronounce her full name, his adorable little mouth only able to spit out "Roz" followed by incomprehensible babble. So she shortens her name to just Roz. Simple enough. But when it comes to naming her kid, she struggles. She starts by assigning him a number, but the gosling doesn't seem happy with it. Eventually, observing that his bill is rather bright, she calls him Brightbill. "Is that satisfactory?" Roz asks. "Satisfactory!" Brightbill chirps back. 

In a way, Roz and Brightbill gave each other their names. And they arrived at these names not through any easily traceable logical process, but through a series of mistakes. 
# Eat, Swim, Fly By Fall

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **ROZZUM 7134**: Eat. Swim. Fly by fall. Or...
> 
> *ROZZUM 7134 pretends to die.*
> 
> **ROZZUM 7134**: Aaaaugh...
> 
> **Pinktail:** Patience is the key.
>

ROZZUM 7134, faced with a task she does not have the programming for, asks a real mother for advice: a weary opossum named Pinktail who's probably given birth to more litters than the number of years she's lived. Not wanting to confuse the new mother, Pinktail breaks the task down into three simple steps: make sure the gosing eats, swims and can fly by fall. 

Initially this sounds perfect for the robot, as clean and unambiguous as an instruction manual. 

Eat. 

Swim. 

Fly by fall. 

But of course, this is a gross over-simplification. While feeding the gosling is simple (the lush Island is full of tasty worms and plants), making sure the gosling *learns* important survival skills is not so straightforward. ROZZUM 7134 was trained to solve problems with logic or, if all else fails, brute force. But this task requires...a gentler touch.

![[Emotional.png]]

Learning is an incredibly emotional process. It means overcoming the pain of failure multiple times just to attain the most basic level of competency at something. Failing to manage our emotions, we end up giving up and making no progress. Adults (mostly) can learn things on their own because they have the *emotional intelligence* to deal with failure. But a newborn child is still running off the most basic programming of avoiding pain. When they inevitably fail, they won't push through unless they have the guidance and encouragement of an adult.

![[Guidance.png]]

At the beginning of their relationship, both Roz and Brightbill lack emotional intelligence. In Roz's case, it's because she doesn't have any emotions. For Brightbill, it's because he has too many. Yet somehow Roz has to get Brightbill through the very emotional process of learning to grow up. A child raising a child.

In order for Roz to successfully complete her task, she has to do that cliched thing that all robots do in stories like this: learn how to *feel*. 

For Roz, learning a new *physical* skill is simple. She only needs to observe something once before being able to mimic it. Whether it be climbing sideways like a crab, or bounding gracefully through the trees like a deer, the learning process is immediate and painless. 

The question is: can one learn how to feel by observing and mimicking the actions of others?
# You Should Not Feel Anything

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Rummage**: May I run a diagnostic? 
> 
> **Roz**: I feel fine.
> 
> **Rummage**: You should not *feel* anything at all.

(Please note that I'm not a neuroscientist, so everything I'm about to say in the following section is just a monkey trying to comprehend something it can't fully understand.)

The most fundamental component for human intelligence is the neuron, a type of cell in our brain that is responsible for sending and receiving messages. When a neuron sends a message, we tend to say that it "fires", like a gun (presumably because of the incredible speed at which these mesages travel). For years scientists have learned how the brain works by putting test subjects into specific situations and identifying which neurons are "fired". For example, if a lot of neurons in a certain area of the brain "fire" when we move our arms, that area is probably related to motor function. 

Neurons can serve many types of functions. One type that has gotten a lot of attention is the "mirror neuron". Put simply, mirror neurons fire exactly the same way under two different circumstances: when we observe an action, and when we perform the same action ourselves. So, as an example, our brain responds the same way whether *we* are eating or watching *someone else* eat.  

While there's nothing conclusive about it yet, there are a lot of theories suggesting that mirror neurons could be the key to learning, empathy, complex language and even self-awareness. In short, it might be the basis for our humanity (a really bold claim that still requires more evaluation).

At the most basic level, mirror neurons allow us to, you guessed it, "mirror" the actions of another person. For example, if you do a silly face or stick your tongue out at an infant, there's a chance that the baby will copy your facial expression.

![[Mirror1.png]]

But the theory is that when we "mirror" someone, we go beyond mere copying. Somehow, we understand the intention or emotion behind that action, and learn how to use it in our own lives. 

So if you're a parent and you spend a lot of time smiling at your new little human, the baby might smile back at you despite not understanding what the smile means. But then, the act of smiling somehow triggers a positive feeling in their head. Therefore, they learn to associate smiling with positive emotion. The baby understands that whenever their parent is smiling, it's because they are happy. And so the baby learns to express its own joy through a smile (or to charm people into giving them what they want).

![[Cute.png]]

In short, mirror neurons allow us to understand what other people are feeling. We can observe an action and instinctively know (and feel) the associated emotion.

If we watch someone getting their teeth pulled out at the dentist, it somehow feels unbearable. If we watch a cockroach crawl up someone's arm, our own skin crawls. If we observe someone crying, we might feel the urge to cry ourselves.

The same holds true when we watch films, which (if they're doing their job) makes us feel as if we have stepped into the shoes of the characters. The best action films make us feel the impact of every punch. The best dramas make us feel the weight of each relationship. The best horror films fill us with the dread of impending death (even if we are in the safety of our own home). 

Which brings us back to The Wild Robot. If Roz can perfectly mirror the action of another living being, can she understand the emotion driving that action *through* her mimicry?

![[Mimic1.png]]

Brightbill, upon first hatching, takes his first glimpse of the world and what he sees are the gleaming blue eyes of ROZZUM 7134. And at that moment, as far as he's concerned, Roz *is* the entire world. And, in the purest expression of love, he presses his little face against hers. But is it really love? We later learn that goslings imprint on the first thing they see. Real-life geese *have* been shown to literally "fall in love" with something as silly as a scientist's boots. While their "reason" might be programmed into them, I believe the resulting emotion is still quite real. I believe in that one simple, earth-shattering moment, Brightbill was programatically expressing a very real love. And Roz, somehow, feels it too. 

![[Pure Expression of Love.png]]

It's important that this isn't the very first animal that Roz encountered on the Island. So far, every single animal has either run away from her or tried to murder her. Although Roz is still emotionally bare, she knows enough to understand these reactions to be negative. [Just like how a cat feels threatened even if you yell profanities at it that it cannot understand](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=23Te7QAsH6w). But along comes Brightbill, who does something that no other animal has ever done before: voluntarily engaging in prolonged physical contact. Even if Roz doesn't know what love is, she can understand that there's something special about this, something completely *new*. It causes her entire body to glow brilliantly in a way it has never done before, her core exploding into colorful shapes, as if some beautiful defect has compromised her entire system.

![[Glow.png]]

And for the rest of her life, Roz will associate this simple action with this strange sensation.

Near the end of the film, when Roz has finally "learned" how to feel, we see her "mirror" the same action, except this time with complete understanding and acceptance of what it means. Even the shot itself is a literal mirror of the first one.

![[Mimic.png]]

The Wild Robot doesn't waste time on the question of, "Will the robot learn how to feel?" We already know she will. The filmmakers instead put all their efforts into telling the story of *how* the robot learns to feel, to ensure that it happened in a natural and organic way. 

And it feels real because Roz learns the same way we learn, through the act of "mirroring". Or, as the saying goes, "fake it till you make it". 

Roz's programming compels her to complete the task of raising Brightbill, but she doesn't have the programming to be a mother. What she is programmed to do is to mimic, to pretend. And so she *pretends* to be a mother, doing all the things that other mothers are expected to do without fully understanding it. Building a nest, finding food, lying about the uncomfortable truths of our harsh and unforgiving reality... You know, typical mom stuff. And in the process of pretending to be a mother, Roz eventually *becomes* a mother, acquiring all the emotional baggage that comes with such a role.   

There's no clear cut-off point where you can say, "Oh, this is *the* moment where Roz becomes a mother." There's no clear turning point where her voice just starts to distinctly become more human (an idea which was in the original script but thankfully changed for the final performance). Rather, there's a weird back and forth that goes on throughout most of the film where Roz can't seem to figure out what she is.

![[Confused.png]]

She refuses to refer to Brightbill as her son most of the time, and when he accuses her of taking care of him out of guilt she defensively says that she needed a task. 

Later, Brightbill verbally tells her that her task is over and that she can just leave, which means that her programming should no longer oblige her to take care of him. Yet she just doubles down and puts in even more effort getting him in the air. 

On the day he is to leave on migration and they are likely to be parted forever, Roz seems cold and distant. "I guess it's time," Brightbill says. "Yes," Roz replies. "Will you be here when I get back?" He asks. "No," she says without elaborating. Yet when he does finally take off, and she checks off the final item on her task list, she suddenly rushes to the edge of the island just to get a final glimpse of him.

Is she taking care of Brightbill out of obligation from her programming, or is it something more? Does her joy come from checking requirements off of a list or seeing her child grow stronger? Is her inability to verbally express love because of a genuine lack of it, or because she is afraid of being vulnerable? What makes Roz's struggle feel so compelling is the fact that real humans with real hearts can also suffer from these doubts. 

![[Conflict.png]]

As mentioned before, a generative AI can only "fake it" but can never "make it". It can mimic love, but it will never gain an understanding of love through its mimicry. It can study billions of images of facial expressions to approximate empathy, but it can never be truly empathic. But Roz is not ChatGPT. She's us. We start off faking the person we're trying to be, and then one day we realize we've been that person for longer than we've thought. We can't point to that exact moment when things changed, when the role became reality. It just kind of happened along the way. 

# How Life Works

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Longneck**: The accident that killed your family, saved you.
> **Brightbill**: I-I never thought of it that way.
> **Longneck**: Funny, how life works.

Five crates floated onto the Island one fateful day, each one containing a Universal Dynamics robot. Four of these crates were smashed against rocks and destroyed. One of them washed up safely on the shores of a beach. ROZZUM 7134 emerged from that crate, not knowing that she was the sole survivor of a terrible accident, and cheerfully marched into the forest to seek out her first task.

Weeks later, a goose's nest was destroyed by a falling robot. Just one egg survived, the runt with the smallest chance of surviving, shielded from death by the bodies of its parents and siblings, who were now all dead. Brightbill emerged from that egg, not knowing that he was the sole survivor of a terrible accident, and cheerfully followed the monster that had just caused that accident into the forest.

In order for Roz and Brightbill to live, so much had to go wrong. So much had to go wrong in order for something to finally go right. How does one make sense of that? How can one be sure that it was all worth it in the end?

Over the course of the film, things continue to go "wrong". A robot, who was never programmed to be a parent or supposed to be in the wilderness, raises a runt who was never supposed to grow up. A fox becomes a father to the gosling he was trying to eat. A wise old goose is killed by a robot, yet his place is taken by the goose who was raised by a robot. A supernatural blizzard takes the lives of many on the Island, but those that remain are saved by a fire created by a robot. Later, another robot sets fire to the forest, only for it to be stopped by the collective efforts of a group of animals who've spent most of their lives killing each other.

It feels like a lot of things go wrong, yet from a certain point of view a lot of things also went right. So much happens that it's hard to tell the balance. 

As the saying goes, we live life forwards but can only understand it backwards. Looking back, the mess of our lives somehow coheres into something beautiful even if we were just trying to get by every day. 

Life is a series of coincidences and chance encounters. Even the most advanced computer model that has been fed the entirety of all human experience could not predict with 100% accuracy the twists and turns of an individual life. It's too random, too imperfect. 

# Try And Fix It

> [!Quote] From "The Wild Robot"
> **Brightbill:** What happened was not your fault. But what you did to try and fix it, is everything.

Although Roz is technically the "hero" of the story, her presence on the Island turns out to have more than a few negative repercussions.

 ![[Disruption.png]]

Upon her arrival, she disrupts the environment with her noisy advertising and destructive attempts at "helping" the local animals (from handing out free stickers to destroying a beaver's dam). 

Her clumsiness eventually leads to an accident that kills an entire family of geese. Although she raises the sole survivor as her own child, she passes on some of her own robotic nature to him, which causes him to be an outcast among his own people.

And worst of all, by activating her transmitter she alerts the humans of the location of the Island, which leads to a massive forest fire at the climax of the film. Although the forest is saved, the Island is now vulnerable to further damage from the humans.

![[Fire.png]]

The thing is, Roz isn't directly responsible for all the terrible things that happen in the film, but at the same time none of these things would have happened if she had never crashed onto the Island. 

Roz can't help but be the product of a destructive system. A system that destroyed most of the world for the sake of comfort and profit. ("Can this world get any better?", asks an advertisment for ROZZUM units...in a world that has been mostly submerged underwater). Roz isn't responsible for this destruction, but she owes her "life" to the system that caused it. And yet, in the face of this uncomfortable truth, she still tries to fix it anyway. 

She killed Brightbill's family but chose to raise him. The same technological progress that created her also led to the blizzard that killed many of the Island's inhabitants, and yet she still uses her advanced technology to save the survivors and keep them alive through the long winter. At the end of the film, she knows going back to her creators won't solve everything but at least it will keep the Island safe for now.

![[Underwater.png]]

Brightbill tells Roz that what happened to his family was not her fault. But what she did to *try* and fix it was "everything". He doesn't tell her that she *fixed* everything. Brightbill will always be a little broken, a little out of place. The world is forever scarred by the work of her creators. It's the fact that she tried that *is* everything.

As humans, we are also products of a destructive system. Especially those of us fortunate enough to enjoy middle-class lives in first-world cities. We aren't *directly* responsible for the damage being done to the world, but at the same time we can live the way we do *because* of it. It's an uncomfortable truth that we sometimes have to ignore just to live with ourselves. We might say that this destruction started long before we were born, and that we are too small to do anything about it. And it's true, we can't fix everything. The damage has been done, and continues to be done. What's the use if I cut down my electricity usage? What's the point of reducing the amount of trash we produce? Big corporations still produce millions of useless products and the rich still live disgustingly wasteful lives. 

We can't fix everything. But trying to fix it is everything. No matter how stupid and imperfect that might be. It's better than taking the easy way out, living in mindless comfort.

# To Survive 

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **Roz**: I know you all have instincts that keep you alive. But sometimes, to survive, we must become more than we were programmed to be.

Programming is a set of clearly defined rules. For example, a lot of computer programs just boil down to a lot of if-statements. If this happens, do that. If that happens, do this. 

But if something unexpected happens, and the program goes through all its if-statements and can't find any corresponding action...it dies. 

Okay, "crashes" is probably the more accurate term, but from the perspective of a computer program that is basically death. A program only lives as long as it is running. Unless the programmer has hastily added in a routine for handling exceptions, although this usually boils down to just resetting the program which is, once again, a kind of death.

Living beings are also programmed to do certain things, under certain circumstances. *How* exactly we are programmed is a matter of scientific debate. *Who* programmed us is a matter of theological debate. But what we do know is that unlike a computer program, our "code" is not fixed. It is being rewritten every single day, whether we realize it or not. 

A lot of the time we are being re-written by forces outside of ourselves. Whether it's the big corporations running the Internet, the expectations of society or the people who surround us every day. Or maybe our "code" becomes corrupted when functions that once served us well in our prehistoric environment start going haywire in the modern world, like when our sensitivity to threats (which would have been very useful in a wilderness filled with predators) causes us to become eaten up by the smallest things.

![[Re-programming.png]]

Yet we also have the ability to override our programming on our own, and the key is being *aware*. To stop and think. To question the world that we were born into. Acting according to our programming is the same as acting without thinking, continuing to do the things the same way they have always been done.

There's evidence that in ancient times, humans spent centuries stubbornly using the same tools before a better alternative was found. It's not that they were lazy or lacked imagination. It's just that breaking out of our routines is really, really hard. 

Sometimes it's easier to stay in a miserable but familiar situation rather than take the plunge into the terrifying unknown. Sometimes, in order for new ideas to be born, you need to introduce a rogue element into the environment. Someone who has not spent their whole lives living in quiet desperation under a flawed system. Someone who can't fall into routine because they never had the chance to develop one. Like a certain clueless Universal Dynamics robot, fresh out of her crate.

![[Think.png]]

Roz is scrambling to get by from day one. 

In The Talos Principle, a fantastic video game that explores philosophical concepts related to artificial intelligence, a scientist muses on what it means to create true intelligence. In her words:

"*Intelligence is more than just problem solving. Intelligence is questioning the assumptions you're presented with. Intelligence is the ability to question existing thought constructs. If we don't make that part of the simulation, all we'll create is a really effective slave.*"

In other words, a true intelligence can choose not to accept what it has been given. 

When Roz first arrives on the Island, the ground rules are laid down pretty clearly. Predators attack prey, prey hide from predators. Relationships are defined simply by whether the other party will eat you or not. It's not a pretty system, but it's worked for as long as the animals remember. The problem is that the world is changing. Oceans are rising. The land is shrinking. Winters are colder. The programming that once served these animals well is incompatible with these unexpected cirumstances. 



Sometimes when I read the comments of film reviews on YouTube, I see commenters joking that they're so happy that the reviewer released this video so that they can finally know what to think about a film. But I suspect that they are only half-joking. Have we all been programmed into content consumption machines that receive so much input every day that we are too fatigued to produce any original thought? Is this why AI creation tools are so popular now? At the click of a button we can instantly get the "pride" of creating something rather than having to go through the arduous process of making the mess in our heads into something tangibly real.

Being what we were programmed to be means taking the easy route. Had Roz done what was expected of her, she would have activated her transmitter and returned to the factory, where she would have been assigned to a life where she always knew exactly what she had to do. She would never have had to suffer everything she goes through in this film. She wouldn't have to go through the physical pain of being beaten and violated by wild animals, or the emotional pain of watching her child fly away with so much left unsaid. She wouldn't make so many mistakes as she stumbled through an environment that she was never meant to inhabit. 

But Roz didn't do what she was programmed for. By the end of the film, her body is covered in scars and wildflowers, and she is more beautiful than ever. She is alive.

# But I Still Heard You

> [!Quote] From The Wild Robot
> **Brightbill:** No, they’ll take your memories. They’ll take *you*.
> 
> **Roz:** Remember when you talked to me, on the ship? They cut my power. *But I still heard you.* Because I was listening with a different part of myself. Wherever that is, that’s where I keep *me*.

One of the most well-known tropes in storytelling is the "Deus ex Machina", meaning "God from the machine". It is defined as "a [plot device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_device "Plot device") whereby a seemingly unsolvable problem in a story is suddenly or abruptly resolved by an unexpected and unlikely occurrence". Which makes me think of a certain moment from The Wild Robot. 

After being kidnapped onto a spaceship by the villainess VONTRA, Roz has her memories wiped and is shut down. Brightbill discovers her body and desperately attempts to revive her, only to be greeted by his own reflection in her lifeless eyes. Crawling into his favorite position on her neck, he tells her all the things he had meant to say but never had the maturity to admit.

![[Tomb.png]]

Roz is dead. Her power is cut. Her memories wiped. There's no way she could possibly hear him. Even if she were revived, she should have no memory of this strange little goose perched on her shoulder. 

But then...The music swells...Glowing hexagons start lighting up all around Roz's core... Brightbill is bathed in a warm, loving glow...He looks in shock as Roz's eyes open and she says to him, in a soft and tender voice, "I love you too."

You've seen this before. You've seen this a million times, done in a million different ways. It's the power of "true love" to transcend the barrier between life and death, to pierce the veil of multiple realities, to defy the mechanics of our physical world. Or at least, defy the mechanics of the world established within the fiction.

Earlier in the film, it's clearly established how Roz gets her power. Either through her power core, or, in case that is damaged, her solar-charged batteries. When Brightbill finds her, neither are working. No power, no Roz. 

There's a fictional philosophical principle known as The Talos Principle (taken from the video game of the same name) that proposes that man is no different from a machine, a machine that relies on the functioning of physical, material systems to live. No matter how amazing a person is, they cannot exist without their physical body. No hearbeat, no consciousness.

Of course one might ask, "But what about the soul?" To which the pretentious philosopher would have a billion retorts. "What is a soul? Can it be measured or observed? Who has one? Does the fly that you just swatted have a soul? Does the plant on your porch have a soul? Does your beat-up old sedan have a soul?" 

Even among people who *do* believe in a soul, what exactly qualifies as having a soul is fiercely debated. In a classic scene from The Iron Giant (perhaps the grandfather of the modern "emotional robot" film), a soul is defined as "something inside of all good things". A simple line, but with complicated implications.  

Can something be made without a soul, and yet gain one through the process of doing good? Can the passionate work of human hands infuse something with a soul?

Certain cultures believe that everything in this world is some representation of the divine. They treat even the most mundane tools (like cups and broomsticks) with the greatest care and respect, as if they too had souls. This is literally "Deus ex Machina": believing that God can reside in even a cold and lifeless machine.   

The Wild Robot makes no attempt at explaining how Roz came back to life. Brightbill doesn't physically repair Roz. She isn't jolted back to life by a bolt of lightning. VONTRA isn't moved by Brightbill's love to reactivate her. No, she just powers on by *herself* because she *"heard"* Brightbill. Even when all her mechanical systems had failed, somehow she was still conscious. 

Most of us don't believe in miracles. Yet the best films can present moments that don't make sense and cause even the non-religious to temporarily throw out all reason and feel something powerful, something that can't be explained.

In the filmmaker's commentary, they note that Roz's mysterious "glowing core" never gets explained and doesn't make sense, but they never got any notes about it from producers or test screenings.  In their words, "...*we get emotionally swept up in it...like, something happened. And it was...magic.*" 

And that's just it. I've struggled to put into words why The Wild Robot gets away with telling a mostly predictable story that makes unexplainable leaps and uses cheap tricks to manipulate the audience's emotions. Discarding the possibility that I'm just an easily manipulated "normie", it can only be what the filmmakers said in their commentary: magic. 

A group of experienced human storytellers took these familiar story blocks, put them together and infused it with something good. The mix of beautiful animation, stirring music and voice actors going way too hard for a kid's movie result in something that, somehow, becomes more than a cold and lifeless artificial construct. It has a soul.

# The Look of the Film
When pitching "Lilo and Stich" to Disney, Chris Sanders created a booklet full of his own hand-drawn illustrations that were good enough to convince the studio to approve the film. “The decision is that, yes, we want to make this movie,” executive Thomas Schumacher told him, “but on one condition: it has to look like you drew it.” The resulting film, which Chris Sanders co-directed with frequent collaborator Dean Deblois, is something that stands out amongst Disney's classics. The soft and warm backgrounds of Hawaii and the adorable round shapes of its cast (Lilo and Sitch are hardly Cindarella and Prince Charming) lend it a personal touch that make it Disney's most intimate film.

Who would have guessed that Chris Sanders and Dean Deblois would then go on to make Dreamworks' most epic film to date. When I saw "How to Train Your Dragon" for the first time, it felt like a cinematic revelation. In 1978, Richard Donner's Superman made audiences believe a man could fly. In 2010, How to Train Your Dragon made me *feel* like I could fly (no 4DX chairs required), with all the wonder and terror that entailed. It's all thanks to the film's mix of realistic camerawork and lighting with stylized movement and character design, a tight brew of opposing elements that was positively addicting. It was unlike any animated film I had seen before, and to this day stands out among Dreamworks' consistently inconsistent library of films.    

The thing about both "Lilo" and "Dragons" is that on first glance, they don't look that groundbreaking to us general audiences. "Lilo" looks like any other 2D animated Disney film, and "Dragons" seems to inherit Shrek's "ugly realism". It's only once you watch the films in full do you realize that something feels different. You become aware of the quiet revolutions behind the screen.

With 2018's Into the SpiderVerse confidently ushering in a new era of hyperstylized big-budget Western animation, The Wild Robot hardly seems groundbreaking. 
****
The Wild Robot does not even try to hide the fact that a lot of its backgrounds are just still paintings (this makes it quite reminiscient of Lilo and Stitch, where background characters were frequently frozen in place).  
****
It may not be a well-known fact, but The Wild Robot is something of a swansong for Dreamworks Animation. Not because they're shutting down, but because they're transitioning to a new business model where they'll be outsourcing a significant portion of their animation work to outside studios. The Wild Robot is the last major in-house production for Dreamworks, and upon the film's completion, many of the animators who worked on it were laid off. I can't say for sure, but it feels like the animators had some idea that this could potentially be their last big project and put everything they had into it. 

An interesting tidbit about Chris Sanders and Dean DeBlois (that I learned from this incredible documentary about the making of Lilo and Stitch) is that they didn't start out as animators (or at least they didn't spend a long time in that position). Most of their careers were spent in the story department. They were the ones sketching storyboards and fine-tuning the overall flow of a scene, not the ones meticulously drawing in the details of every frame. They're very open about this. There's a behind-the-scenes clip where Chris Sanders is visiting the office of one of The Wild Robot's animators, who's in the process of painting ripples onto a body of water (which most viewers will probably never notice). Pointing at the animator's screen, Sander candidly says, "I can't do that." Although his illustrations and descriptions gave the team a consistent vision to follow, the work of hundreds of artists brought the vision to life. And true to what Thomas Schumacher said, it looks exactly like he drew it.
